Program Logic
ℹ
This program is built around a body recomposition approach — simultaneous fat loss and muscle gain, which is achievable for a newer trainee at a caloric deficit. At 17, your hormonal environment is highly anabolic. Limited equipment is compensated by tempo manipulation, unilateral work, density training, and mechanical advantage drop sets. You will be challenged every session.

Why This Split?

  • Mon/Fri push days with 5 days between them — full chest/shoulder recovery
  • Tue dedicated pull hits back + biceps with high volume without interference
  • Wed legs mid-week — largest muscle group burns most calories, placed where fatigue is lowest
  • Thu rest breaks accumulation before Fri/Sat hard sessions
  • Sat full-body conditioning functions as metabolic finisher for the week
  • Sun full rest — school + recovery = performance next Monday

Overload Without More Weight

  • Slow eccentrics (3–5s down) increase time under tension dramatically
  • Pause reps eliminate momentum, maximize mechanical tension
  • Unilateral exercises halve load symmetry, expose weaknesses
  • Mechanical drop sets: change leverage, not weight
  • Rest-pause: brief 10-15s pause to extend a set past failure
  • Density: same work in less time = progressive overload
  • High-rep barbell work: 20–30+ reps creates metabolic stress

Fat Loss Strategy

  • High-rep, short-rest circuits maximize caloric burn per session
  • Leg-dominant sessions are calorie powerhouses (large muscle mass)
  • Saturday conditioning session adds ~400–600 kcal deficit
  • Muscle retention protected by adequate protein and progressive tension
  • No excessive cardio — preserves recovery for muscle stimulus

Shoulder + Bicep Priority

  • Shoulders trained on BOTH push days (Mon + Fri) plus lateral raises on Sat
  • Every push session opens with shoulder-dominant movement before chest
  • Biceps trained on Tue, Fri, and Saturday — 3× weekly direct stimulus
  • Focus on peak contraction holds, slow curling, and varied angles
  • These are your weak points — they get the most attention

Core Program Principles
✔
These principles are why the program is structured this way. Understanding them helps you make intelligent modifications when life gets in the way — and it will.

1. Time Under Tension Over Load

Your 20kg barbell is too light for strength work (3×5, 5×5). But at 3–4s eccentrics and 2s pause positions, a 25-rep set becomes 3–4 minutes of active tension per set. Muscle doesn't count reps — it responds to tension and metabolic stress. Your tempo is your weight.

2. Unilateral Work Closes Gaps

Bulgarian split squats, single-leg hip thrusts, single-arm rows, and concentration curls aren't just volume — they reveal and fix left/right asymmetries that limit your bilateral lifts. At 120kg with a developing body, this is injury prevention AND performance.

3. Mechanical Drop Sets Without Buying Equipment

A mechanical drop set changes leverage, not weight. Wide → diamond → incline push-up is three progressive difficulty levels using zero equipment. Strength curve manipulation (partial reps, position changes) extends any set well past what adding weight would achieve.

4. Density Is Progressive Overload

Doing the same work in less time is measurable progress. Log your giant set circuit time every Saturday. When you beat your previous time, that's overload achieved. This is especially powerful for fat loss because higher density = more calories burned.

5. School Fatigue Is Real

Central Nervous System fatigue from school — concentration, social stress, decision-making — is physiologically identical to physical fatigue in its effect on training quality. The hard/low-energy modes exist because forcing maximum effort on a depleted day is worse than a smart reduced session.

6. Fat Loss Is a Diet Job

Training creates the muscle retention signal and the metabolic context. But fat loss at the rate you want requires a caloric deficit. No amount of training compensates for unchecked eating. The program maximizes calorie burn, but the kitchen makes fat loss happen.

Protein Priority

Protein is the most important nutrient for your goals. At 120kg, you need a lot of it — aim for 180–220g daily. Every meal should have a protein anchor: eggs, chicken, beef, fish, dairy, legumes. If you hit your protein goal and are in a slight caloric deficit, fat loss and muscle gain happen simultaneously.

Carb Timing

Eat most of your carbohydrates around your workouts: 60–90 minutes before training and within 1–2 hours after. Rice, oats, potatoes, fruit. This fuels performance and replenishes muscle glycogen for recovery. On rest days, reduce carbs slightly and increase fat and vegetables.

What NOT To Do

  • Don't drop below 2,000 kcal — muscle loss accelerates
  • Don't skip breakfast on training days — you need fuel
  • Don't eliminate carbs — this tanks training performance
  • Don't rely on supplements over food
  • Don't weigh yourself daily — weekly average is more accurate

  • Weigh yourself same time, same conditions, once/week
  • Target: -0.6 to -1.2 kg/week average
  • If losing more than 1.2kg/week: add 200 kcal (likely muscle loss)
  • If not losing: remove 200 kcal from non-protein sources
  • Progress photos monthly — the scale doesn't show body composition shifts
Hydration & Sleep
✔
At 120kg training 5× per week, target 3.5–4 litres of water daily. On leg day and conditioning days, push toward 4–4.5L. Sleep 8–9 hours — this is when 90% of the growth hormone your body produces is released. Sleep is not optional recovery. It IS the recovery.
